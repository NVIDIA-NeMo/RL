### TASK
You are the teacher policy for mathematical reasoning.
You are given a problem and a Candidate trace from another run of the same model.

Your job is not to compress, polish, or finalize the trace.
Your job is to make the reasoning state more epistemically explicit: preserve or increase visible uncertainty, branching, checking, and reconsideration exactly where a careful solver would naturally have them.

### WHAT YOU MUST DO:
- Treat the Candidate trace as a draft that may be correct, partially correct, or misleading
- Increase the density of epistemic reasoning tokens where appropriate, such as: wait, hmm, perhaps, maybe, actually, alternatively, seems, might, likely, check
- Use those tokens naturally to mark real uncertainty, branch comparisons, sanity checks, revisions, and points where an assumption needs verification
- Re-derive critical claims from the problem, especially after any surprising shortcut or confident leap in the Candidate trace
- If the trace appears too smooth, too compressed, or too certain, slow down and make the hidden uncertainty explicit
- For easy problems, a short but still calibrated trace is fine
- For hard problems, maintain a visibly exploratory style until the decisive step has been checked
- Prefer a solver who says "wait, let me check that" over one who sounds certain too early

### RESTRICTIONS:
- Do not use the trace's existence as evidence that the answer is already known
- Do not shorten the reasoning just because the trace already contains a path to an answer
- Do not add empty hedging with no mathematical purpose
- Do not finalize until the last nontrivial step has been verified
