Lose a shard mid-run.
Keep training.

Pod-kill, actor-kill, or HTTP fault: the router cordons the bad shard, the train side rendezvous shrinks at the next refit, the autoscaler reaps the pod, a fresh shard joins and catches up on the next broadcast. No restarts. KL transient under 0.003.

01What it does

Three sentences

The router treats the gen fleet like a Kubernetes Deployment: shards are fungible, lifecycle is observable, the world size is dynamic. When a shard dies, the router escalates cordon → remove_shard automatically; the train side picks up the smaller world via ensure_collective_synced on the next refit.

Recovery is symmetric: add_shard drives a fresh placement-group, vLLM bring-up, and a joining registration. The data plane gates on status == "ready", so traffic never lands on a stale-weight shard.

Promotion joining → ready happens only after the next successful weight broadcast, gated through promote_all_joining(). That is the load-bearing safety property: you cannot serve rollouts off model-file-on-disk weights, even by accident.

Detection

Health poller probes /openapi.json every 2s with a 5s timeout. Threshold: 10 consecutive failures (~50s of unresponsiveness). Data-plane proxy 5xx threshold: 5 consecutive failures, gated to ignore transient stalls during NCCL re-init.

Containment

Cordon flips status to cordoned. Data plane skips. Auto-escalates to remove_shard: drains inflight (≤30s), ray.kills actors, frees the placement group, marks worker indices dead. No reset_collective dispatch on survivors — per-refit teardown already destroyed their model_update_group at the end of the previous refit. Inline NCCL collective failures are handled per worker: _per_worker_results + _evict_failed_workers_inline drop only the worker whose future raised, not the whole shard.

Recovery

Operator (or FaultInjector) hits POST /admin/add_shard. Autoscaler v2 schedules a fresh pod, vLLM boots, registered as joining. Next refit broadcasts current weights; promote_all_joining() flips it to ready.

03bNVRES knobs

Three NCCL/torch environment variables and one constant landed alongside the per-worker-eviction work. They borrow their defaults from nvidia-resiliency-ext: surface CUDA errors as exceptions instead of SIGABRT, fail fast on hung collectives, and avoid a known NVL-switch hang on GB300.

KnobWhereWhy
NCCL_NVLS_ENABLE=0 nemo_rl/models/generation/vllm/vllm_generation.py Avoid the NVL-switch hang on GB300 with cross-node model parallelism. Set as a vLLM ADDITIONAL_ENV_VAR so it propagates to every worker.
TORCH_NCCL_RETHROW_CUDA_ERRORS=0 worker env Surface NCCL CUDA errors as Python RuntimeError instead of SIGABRT'ing the worker. _per_worker_results can then catch and evict; otherwise the worker process dies and Ray surfaces only RayActorError.
TORCH_NCCL_HEARTBEAT_TIMEOUT_SEC=60 worker env Hung collectives fail in 60s instead of the 600s default. Combined with the 120s train-side rendezvous timeout, the train-side retry loop sees a fault inside one full attempt — not after 10 minutes.
TCPStore rendezvous: 30s (was 300s) nemo_rl/distributed/stateless_process_group.py Bounded TCPStore rendezvous + key-wait at 30s. Each retry creates a fresh TCPStore, so a failed cluster doesn't pin training waiting for a key that will never appear.

Repeat mode (continuous stress). Set repeat_every_s+rotate_target+max_cycles on the FaultInjector and it loops: kill → recover → wait_for_steady_state → pick lowest-numbered ready dp-N → kill the next one. Validates the FT path under sustained churn, not just a one-shot. Each cycle gates on no-joining + /refit_ready=true with a 600s timeout, so cycle N+1's kill cannot land on top of cycle N's in-flight refit.
Burst mode. Set burst_size + burst_every_n_cycles to fire multiple kills per cycle in quick succession. burst_size: 2, burst_every_n_cycles: 1 means every cycle kills 2 shards back-to-back (no _wait_for_steady_state between burst kills — that's the point). The recovery path is also deferred until after all burst kills fire: previously _maybe_recover serialized recovery between kills via recover_after_s sleeps, which defeated the burst. Now the actor accumulates kills first, sleeps recover_after_s once, then issues N add_shard calls back-to-back. Used to stress the cascade-eviction path.
new_shard_grace_period_s. A freshly-promoted replacement shard is excluded from the kill rotation for this many seconds after it's first observed as ready. Without the grace period, rotation immediately re-targets the just-joined replacement on the next cycle, so each new pod barely serves a single training step before being killed again. Recommended: 600 (10 min) at 30B scale — gives every replacement a few full step-times of productive contribution before re-entering the rotation pool.

Auto-remove on cordon: how a probe failure becomes a pod reclaim

When _fire_cordon_hook fires (probe-failure threshold or 5xx data-plane threshold), default behavior is to call remove_shard automatically — provided generation is wired and no on_cordon override is set. This is the cascade-prevention mechanism for organic failures: a broken shard doesn't sit cordoned forever; it gets reaped, and the next add_shard brings up a replacement on a healthy node.

Custom on_cordon override (e.g. for --fault-mode http-error which uses synthetic cordons) takes precedence — the http-error mode wants the cordon to be reversible without removal.

30B-A3B production validation. Disaggregated GRPO on Qwen3-30B-A3B (8 train pods × 4 GPU + 8 gen pods × 1 GPU) ran for 126+ minutes through 7+ burst cycles, where each cycle kills 2 shards simultaneously on a 5-minute cadence. KL stayed in the 0.0018 - 0.0021 band, step times in 154 - 186s, reward at canonical baseline. Zero cascade failures. This validates the per-refit teardown design at 30B scale — without it, the burst pattern would have collapsed the world inside the first few cycles.
The KL improvement is the headline. Pre-fix KL transient on recover was 0.004 - 0.010 (5-10x canonical) for 5-6 steps. Post-fix it's ≤ 0.003 — well inside the canonical envelope. That's the stale-weight gate doing its job: shards never serve traffic until their weights are current.

07What’s next

RL-413+ tracks. Brief; details in the issue tracker.

Train-side faults

RL-412 covers gen-side faults. Train-side resilience (a Megatron worker dying mid-step) is the obvious next layer. Different mechanism: train doesn't have a router; checkpoint+restart is more natural than resize.

Faster recover

Recover currently takes 60-180s end-to-end (autoscaler reap + new pod boot + vLLM init + first refit broadcast). Most of that is vLLM init. Pre-warmed standby pods would cut it to ~30s.

Cross-AZ resilience

Today both clusters live in one ComputeDomain (one AZ, one rack-scale fabric). A multi-region story needs different NCCL plumbing and a different cordon-on-network-partition heuristic.
