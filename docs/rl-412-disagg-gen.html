01What it is

Three sentences

RL-412 splits NeMo-RL's GRPO into two long-running RayClusters: one trains, the other generates. A single FastAPI router per gen daemon owns every external interaction — data plane, control plane, lifecycle, weight sync, metrics — on port 8089.

Cross-cluster NCCL (the model_update_group) flows weights from train to gen on every refit; the router orchestrates it.

Crucially, the router can resize the gen fleet mid-run via add_shard/remove_shard, and the train side will rendezvous at the new world size on the next refit. That is the foundation for the fault-tolerance work in RL-412 FT.

04Cross-cluster NCCL

Weight sync travels over a single NCCL group spanning every train rank plus every gen rank: model_update_group. The router owns rendezvous; the train side owns the broadcast.

Rendezvous

Train side picks a master (ip, port) on the train cluster and dispatches init_collective symmetrically: every train rank and every gen rank enters the same NCCL init_process_group with a contiguous WORLD_SIZE = train_ws + gen_ws. Train ranks fill 0..train_ws-1; gen ranks train_ws..train_ws+gen_ws-1.

Each pair takes ~3-8 seconds at this scale. Bounded at 120s per attempt.

Broadcast

Train rank 0 packs params into ping-pong buffers and CUDA-IPC-hands them to RefitWorker, a sibling Ray actor on the same node + same GPU (separate process). RefitWorker NCCL-broadcasts each chunk to every gen rank, then ACKs back so the sender can swap buffers. ~12 chunks of ~5GB each for Qwen3-30B-A3B. The cross-cluster NCCL group lives only inside RefitWorker — the Megatron worker process never touches it, so a queued NCCL error from a dead gen peer can't poison Megatron's training streams.

At the end of every refit — success OR failure — both sides destroy the group: RefitWorker's broadcast_weights_until_complete runs group.destroy() in finally:; vllm_backend.update_weights_from_collective does the same on each gen worker. The placement groups stay up across refits; only the NCCL group is rebuilt by the next init_collective.

Per-refit teardown is the safety property. Between refits there is no NCCL state spanning train↔gen. A gen peer that dies outside the broadcast window has no peer process to poison — the next refit's init_collective rebuilds from scratch on every shard. This is what makes burst kills (multiple shards dying simultaneously, every cycle) recoverable rather than cascading to world=0.
REFIT RefitWorker split-actor architecture

The problem. Hosting model_update_group inside the Megatron worker process meant a gen peer dying mid-broadcast would queue cudaErrorLaunchFailure into rank 0's primary CUDA context. Megatron's EP/TP/PP/DP groups all share that context — a single queued device error could surface on any subsequent CUDA op, anywhere in the training step.

The fix. The cross-cluster group now lives in a sibling Ray actor, RefitWorker, scheduled with hard NodeAffinitySchedulingStrategy onto train rank 0's node and pinned to rank 0's physical GPU via CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICES + RAY_EXPERIMENTAL_NOSET_CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICES=1. num_gpus=0 tells Ray not to allocate a fresh device. Two processes on one GPU, independent primary CUDA contexts, joined by CUDA IPC.

# Cross-cluster world layout (per-refit):
#   rank 0:    RefitWorker      (sibling Ray actor, train rank 0's GPU)
#   ranks 1..N: vLLM gen workers (one per gen pod)
# Megatron policy workers DO NOT participate.

Data path per refit.

  1. All train ranks call megatron_bridge.export_hf_weights to materialize full-precision weights via intra-cluster TP/EP/PP all-gathers. Only rank 0 streams onward.
  2. Rank 0 packs params into one of two GPU "ping-pong" buffers, runs torch.cuda.synchronize(), exports a CUDA IPC handle via reduce_tensor, and sends (handle, [], used_bytes) over ZMQ REQ.
  3. RefitWorker (REP) recv_pyobjs the handle, rebuild_cuda_tensors it (zero-copy view of rank 0's GPU memory — same physical GPU), clone()s into RefitWorker's own allocation, syncs, and frees the IPC view back to the sender.
  4. RefitWorker NCCL-broadcasts the local copy to gen ranks, syncs, ACKs back so the sender can swap to the other ping-pong buffer.
  5. When the sender is done it sends IPCProtocol.COMPLETE; RefitWorker breaks out, returns, and (in finally:) destroys the group.

For Qwen3-30B-A3B that's roughly 12 chunks of ~5GB each, logged as forwarded 12 chunks via RefitWorker on the producer side.

Why this is safe. A peer death only poisons RefitWorker's CUDA context. The Policy ray.kills and respawns RefitWorker at the next refit (lm_policy._ensure_refit_worker liveness ping); Megatron's process is unaffected because it sits behind a process boundary. Combined with the per-refit group.destroy() in RefitWorker's finally: block (mirrored on the gen side in vllm_backend.update_weights_from_collective), there is no NCCL state spanning refits anywhere — not in Megatron, not in RefitWorker, not in vLLM workers.

Source: nemo_rl/models/policy/workers/refit_worker.py, lm_policy._ensure_refit_worker, megatron_policy_worker.broadcast_weights_for_collective (path 1), nemo_rl/utils/packed_tensor.py (packed_broadcast_producer / packed_broadcast_consumer).

How the train side handles a peer that dies during rendezvous

The interesting failure mode is mid-rendezvous: rank K joins NCCL and then disappears. Without a timeout, every other rank blocks on ncclCommInitRank forever.

RL-412 wraps init_collective in a 4-attempt loop with a 120s rendezvous budget per attempt. On timeout: abort_collective on the train side, ray.cancel(force=True) on the futures, sleep 3s for the gen side to settle, re-read /current_gen_world_size (which now reflects the new fault), and retry at the smaller world. After 4 attempts it raises — the run fails loudly rather than silently wedge.

The gen side recovers naturally because per-refit teardown already destroyed every survivor's model_update_group at the end of the prior refit (in vllm_backend.update_weights_from_collective's finally:). There is no leftover NCCL state to abort — the next init_collective rebuilds from scratch at the new (smaller) world. Earlier designs did dispatch reset_collective on survivors from remove_shard/add_shard, but that call turned out to cascade-evict survivors under burst kills (queued behind active NCCL ops, exceeded the 30s timeout, router falsely flagged the slow survivor as poisoned, kicked it, queued reset on the next survivor, etc.) and was removed. See the comment block in generation_router.py at remove_shard for the full failure mode.

Source: nemo_rl/models/generation/remote_generation.py:194 (ensure_collective_synced); nemo_rl/models/generation/generation_router.py (remove_shard NB comment).

PERF NCCL library warmup at vLLM startup

The disagg recipe runs vLLM at TP=1/PP=1/EP=1, so there is no intra-shard NCCL group inside a gen worker's process. The cross-cluster model_update_group is the FIRST NCCL collective the engine_core process ever sees. NCCL's per-process lazy init (dlopen shared libs, IB device probe, NCCL workspace cuMemMap) runs on that first call and adds 15-20s to the first init_collective after a fresh shard joins — which surfaces as a fat step-time spike on the FIRST refit involving a new pod.

VllmGenerationWorker.warmup_nccl_library pre-pays that cost during bring-up by creating a degenerate 1-rank Communicator.init, running a no-op broadcast on a 1-element tensor, and tearing it down. NCCL allows nranks=1 — ops are no-ops, but the per-process state machine runs the same init paths as a real comm. Cost: ~1-2s once per pod, fully hidden by the existing vLLM init.

Wired into both add_dp_worker Phase A (after actor spawn, on the bare actor handle before append_spawned_worker) and prepare_refit_info for the initial cohort. Validated via the nccl_init_timing log line in StatelessProcessGroup: cold-pod first init showed comm_init=17.99s; warmed pod showed ~4-5s. Net effect: first-refit-with-new-pod time dropped from ~18s to ~4-5s.

Source: nemo_rl/models/generation/vllm/vllm_backend.py (warmup_nccl_library), vllm_worker_async.py (warmup_nccl_library_async), vllm_generation.add_dp_worker + prepare_refit_info.

PERF load_format: dummy — skip safetensor load on bringup

vLLM's default bring-up reads model weights from disk (~6-7 minutes for Qwen3-30B-A3B safetensors). On the FT path, every kill triggers an add_shard that provisions a replacement pod, and the joining→ready window directly affects how much steady-state time each FT cycle gets. Six minutes of safetensor load every cycle is the dominant cost in that window.

Setting load_format: dummy in the vLLM config skips the disk read entirely — vLLM allocates randomly-initialized tensors of the right shape/dtype. Bring-up drops from ~6-7min to ~1-2min.

Safe because prepare_refit_info runs immediately after init priming state_dict_info, and the FIRST refit broadcast lands on correctly-shaped (junk-valued) buffers and overwrites them in-place. The data plane gates on status == "ready", and a joining shard never serves traffic until promote_all_joining() flips it post-broadcast — so the junk values are never observable to a rollout client.

Source: infra/examples/generation_standalone_qwen3_30b_ft_full.yaml (vllm_cfg.load_format: dummy).

Why only rank 0 (RefitWorker) participates in the cross-cluster comm

An earlier design put every train rank into model_update_group so EP shards could broadcast directly to the corresponding gen rank. That worked but tied every train rank's primary CUDA context to the lifetime of cross-cluster NCCL — one dead gen peer poisoned every Megatron rank.

The current design splits the producer in half. All train ranks run megatron_bridge.export_hf_weights, which performs intra-cluster TP/EP/PP all-gathers to materialize full-precision weights on rank 0. Only rank 0 then forwards the materialized chunks to RefitWorker via CUDA IPC + ZMQ. RefitWorker (a sibling Ray actor sharing rank 0's physical GPU but in its own process) is the sole train-side participant in the cross-cluster model_update_group. Cross-cluster world layout is now {rank 0: RefitWorker, ranks 1..N: gen workers} — Megatron does not touch it.

The trade-off: one extra IPC hop per chunk (~free, same physical GPU, zero-copy). The win: a peer death only poisons RefitWorker's CUDA context. The Policy ray.kills and respawns it at the next refit; Megatron's process is unaffected.

Source: nemo_rl/models/policy/workers/refit_worker.py, lm_policy._ensure_refit_worker, megatron_policy_worker.broadcast_weights_for_collective (path 1).

Why we doubled the 30B train side

The canonical qwen3_30b_math_4n_4gpu shape uses 4 train pods (16 GPUs) with EP=8, DP=2. Step time ran ~50-70s, which made stress-testing the FT path slow.

RL-412 doubles to 8 train pods (32 GPUs); EP=8 stays, DP factor goes 2 → 4. Per-step wall time roughly halves. Same recipe yaml, same Megatron config, just a wider train cluster. See infra/nrl_k8s/examples/qwen3_30b_if_fault_tolerant.gb300.infra.yaml.

The DRA dance — why train and gen share ResourceClaimTemplates

Cross-cluster NCCL on GB300 hits cudaErrorOperationNotPermitted (CUDA error 800) on the IMEX channel device unless both clusters live in the same NVIDIA ComputeDomain. The gen cluster declares the standard compute-domain-channel and roce-channel claims; nrl-k8s auto-creates ResourceClaimTemplates from those.

The train cluster references those templates by name via custom-named claims (shared-compute-domain, shared-roce). The nrl-k8s CLI rewrites template names only for the standard claim names, so by deviating we inherit the gen cluster's templates verbatim. Result: one ComputeDomain, two RayClusters, one fabric.

06What’s next

RL-412 lands the substrate. Several tracks build on it.

Asymmetric scaling

Right now train and gen scale together. Future work: scale gen independently of train when rollouts are the bottleneck (or vice versa). The router already supports add_shard at runtime; the missing piece is a policy that decides when.

Heterogeneous gen

Mix shard types: some H100, some GB300, some on-prem. The router already abstracts the per-shard URL; add_dp_worker needs to accept placement hints.

Multi-tenant gen

One gen cluster fronting many simultaneous training runs. Each refit pushes a different model checkpoint to a partition of shards. The hardest piece is the NCCL group lifecycle — today there's one comm per (train, gen) pair.
